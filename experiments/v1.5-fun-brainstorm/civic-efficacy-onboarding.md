# Sovereign v1.5 — Civic Efficacy + Onboarding (study-swarm findings)

Research question: does the player actually LEARN/CARE about why public credit + a functioning Republic matters (A), and do they get far enough in to find out (B)? Findings below; each ends with a Sovereign implication.

---

## A. CIVIC / HISTORICAL EFFICACY — make the argument felt through mechanics

**A1. Procedural rhetoric: the argument lives in the rules, not the banner.** Bogost (*Persuasive Games*, 2007) defines procedural rhetoric as "the art of persuasion through rule-based representations and interactions rather than the spoken word." Games make claims about how systems work by *modeling* them and inviting players to form judgments through play.
→ *Sovereign:* The thesis ("sound public credit + functioning federal institutions built the Republic") must be a SYSTEM the player operates, not a Chronicler line. Bond yields, the assumption of state debt, and tax-revenue feedback loops should be the load-bearing argument. The player should win by *building the credit system* and lose legibly when it collapses.

**A2. Intrinsic integration is the difference between a real teaching game and "chocolate-covered broccoli."** Habgood & Ainsworth (*Journal of the Learning Sciences*, 2011, "Motivating Children to Learn Effectively") tested *Zombie Division*: the intrinsically-integrated variant (math = the combat mechanic) produced significantly higher learning AND more time-on-task than the extrinsic variant (math bolted onto unrelated play). A 2022 follow-up (Cutting & Iacovides, ACM CHI PLAY) found the gain comes via an *attentional* mechanism: intrinsic design directs attention only to learning-relevant features.
→ *Sovereign:* The history content must BE the core loop's fiction-and-math, never a quiz between turns. If you could strip the Hamilton theme and keep the same game, you've built broccoli. Every number the player optimizes should also be a historical lever (e.g., funding par-vs-discount on the debt = a real fight over who gets paid).

**A3. "Chocolate-covered broccoli" is a documented failure mode and players smell it.** Term coined by Brenda Laurel (*Utopian Entrepreneur*, 2001). The edutainment critique (Edutopia/Farber; Tedium history of Math Blaster): bolting a reward-game onto a drill moves motivation from intrinsic to extrinsic and *reduces* it; "kids sniff out fake fun, and once they've bounced you'll never get them back."
→ *Sovereign:* The win condition cannot be "answer history correctly to unlock fun." Adults bounce from this even faster than kids. The reward for good play must be *better statecraft outcomes*, which are themselves the lesson.

**A4. Strategy/history games teach history as a SYSTEM, not a fact list — and reach the disaffected.** Squire's *Civilization III* classroom studies (*Replaying History*, dissertation 2004; "Changing the Game") found students built "conceptual understandings across world history, geography, and politics… an understanding of history as a process, a logic, a system, rather than a body of content," and that game-based units engaged students who felt alienated by lecture.
→ *Sovereign:* Lean into "history as a process you operate." A player who feels *why* assumption was contentious (states that already paid their debts resent bailing out states that didn't) has learned more than one who memorized 1790. Build the *tensions* as opposing pressures, not as trivia.

**A5. Civic gameplay correlates with real civic engagement — design for the civic verbs.** Kahne, Middaugh & Evans, *The Civic Potential of Video Games* / "Games for Civic Learning: A Conceptual Framework" (MacArthur, 2008/2009): teens whose play involved civic experiences (simulating government, debating social/moral issues, helping others) showed significantly higher real-world civic engagement. The vMOBilize quasi-experiment (254 students, Harvard Kennedy School, 2016) found gameplay raised political efficacy/participation, with the LARGEST gains among the least-engaged at baseline.
→ *Sovereign:* Put the player in genuine civic *roles* — proposing an Act, whipping votes, balancing creditors vs. taxpayers vs. states. The efficacy payoff is strongest for newcomers, which doubles as the case for accessibility (B). Make "I passed a contested Act of Congress and saw the consequence" a felt verb.

**A6. Gee's principles — identity, situated meaning, "performance before competence."** Gee (*What Video Games Have to Teach Us*, 2003) derives 36 learning principles from good games: learners take on a powerful *identity*; meaning is *situated* in action ("the meanings of signs are situated in embodied experience," not decontextualized); good games let players *perform before they are competent* via well-ordered problems and just-in-time information.
→ *Sovereign:* Give the player a named identity (a Treasury role / a member of the First Congress), so abstractions like "public credit" are felt as *your* reputation and *your* Republic's survival. Deliver rules *just-in-time* at the moment of first use, not in a manual.

**A7. The systemic-empathy lever: let institutional failure be FELT, not narrated.** *Papers, Please* and *This War of Mine* are studied as games where "the system responds to the player's own values" and players experience social/institutional breakdown from inside (de Smale et al., *Games and Culture*, 2019; playthepast analysis). The emotional force comes from *consequence under your own choices*, not from a message screen.
→ *Sovereign:* The most persuasive moment is a *credit-collapse spiral the player triggered* — default raises borrowing cost, which starves the Treasury, which forces worse terms, which deepens default. Let the player live one downward turn-loop. That single felt spiral sells "why sound credit matters" better than any paragraph.

**A8. Procedural rhetoric's known limit (Sicart) — back the system with light authored framing.** Sicart, "Against Procedurality" (2011): rules alone don't guarantee the intended reading; players make their own meaning and may miss or subvert the argument. Even Bogost concedes the "procedural enthymeme" — the player completes the argument. Designers often need *explicit framing* so the systemic point lands.
→ *Sovereign:* Mechanics carry the argument (A1), but pair each major system with one *crisp authored beat* (a Chronicler line, an outcome card) that names what just happened — "Without assumption, the Union's credit fractures state by state." Don't over-narrate (the v1.4 voice work governs cadence); use framing as a *pointer to the system the player just felt*, not a substitute for it.

## B. ONBOARDING — get a newcomer to the first satisfying decision before they bounce

**B1. The first ~10 minutes are make-or-break; the tutorial itself is the #1 drop-off point.** Industry retention analyses (Maf.ad; FeatureUpvote; "The $10M Tutorial") and Nacke ("Don't Spook the Newbies," 2023): keep the front-loaded tutorial under ~5 min, *teach by doing* (mechanics embedded in real play, not text walls), and give a skip/fast-forward. Beware the "onboarding cliff" — a great tutorial that dumps the player into full complexity with no support.
→ *Sovereign:* Target a *first meaningful decision inside ~3–5 minutes* — one constrained choice (e.g., "fund the debt at par or at market discount?") with a visible, immediate consequence. No 20-rule preamble. Ramp, don't cliff.

**B2. Progressive disclosure — show only what matters now (Cooper; Nielsen).** Cooper, *About Face*, advocates "a simple face on complex processes," matching the represented model to the user's mental model. Nielsen (NN/g, pattern since 1995): reveal advanced controls on demand; this smooths the novice curve without slowing experts.
→ *Sovereign:* Stage the systems. Turn 1 exposes ONE lever (debt). Bank, taxation, assumption, foreign credit unlock over subsequent turns as the player demonstrates the prior one. The full board exists under the hood; the *interface* reveals it progressively. Provide an always-available "what does this do?" inspector for experts/returning players.

**B3. "Subway legibility" / total information — hide nothing; make the next move readable at a glance.** Into the Breach's designer (Justin Ma, *Designing for Subway Legibility*) and Slay the Spire share a philosophy (PCGamesN: "their greatest trick is that they hide nothing"): enemy intent and the player's counters are fully telegraphed, complexity lives in *clearly defined tools*, and vital info is pulled out of wikis into the UI via tooltips on every element.
→ *Sovereign:* For a deterministic economic game this is the single highest-leverage onboarding move. Telegraph consequences *before commit*: hovering "fund at par" should preview the yield/treasury/political deltas. Determinism is an asset — show the player the cause→effect chain so the *system itself* is the teacher (this also serves A1/A7: you can't feel a spiral you can't see coming).

**B4. Complex games need a longer, gentler ramp AND persistent help past the tutorial.** Retention guidance for strategy/puzzle genres: front tutorial stays short, but introduce complexity *gradually* over the first session and keep small hints + a Help button available so the player is never abandoned. Wingspan's physical "Swift-Start Pack" (curated starter cards + guided first turns) is a board-game exemplar of a hand-held, low-variance opening.
→ *Sovereign:* Ship a "Swift-Start" first scenario — a scripted, low-variance opening (a single historical decision point with pre-set conditions, e.g., the 1790 funding debate) that guarantees a satisfying first "aha," then opens into the full game. Keep a contextual hint layer for the first 2–3 turns, then fade it.

---

### Minimum onboarding to the first "aha" (synthesis of B1–B4)
1. Drop into ONE named role with ONE lever and a clear stake (≤5 min, perform-before-competence — A6/B1).
2. Telegraph the consequence of each option on hover *before* commit (subway legibility — B3).
3. Make the first decision a *real historical tension* with an immediate, legible outcome (intrinsic integration — A2/A5).
4. Reveal the next system only after the player has used the current one (progressive disclosure — B2).
5. Within the first scenario, let the player feel ONE small consequence-loop — ideally a mini credit win, with a collapse spiral reserved for later as the persuasive gut-punch (A7).

### The two highest-order rules for Sovereign
- **Make the civic thesis a system the player wins/loses by — and lets them FEEL a credit/institution spiral (A1+A2+A7).** If the theme is strippable, it's broccoli.
- **Use determinism + total-information telegraphing as the teacher (B3) and disclose systems one lever at a time (B1/B2)** so a newcomer reaches a real historical decision in under five minutes.
