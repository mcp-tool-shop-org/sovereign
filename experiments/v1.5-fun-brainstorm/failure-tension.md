# Felt Tension in a Deterministic Economic Game — Research Findings

**Question:** What actually creates felt TENSION/dread in a deterministic economic game without being punitive, unwinnable, or a death-spiral — and WHY do multi-stage gauges still feel decorative?

**Core diagnosis up front:** Sovereign's failure system is decorative not because thresholds are too high, but because the threat is a **cliff you rarely reach** rather than a **slope you stand on every turn**. The fix is structural, not numeric: convert the failure system from a *rare terminal event* into a *continuous, telegraphed, self-owned drain that compounds*. Lowering thresholds alone (the prior swarm's move) cannot work, because a threat that "rarely fires" is perceptually identical to a threat that doesn't exist.

---

## 1. Loss aversion: the pain of losing is ~2x the joy of gaining (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979)
*Prospect Theory, Econometrica 47(2). https://www.jstor.org/stable/1914185* — Losses loom roughly twice as large as equivalent gains; the value function is convex (risk-seeking) over losses.
**Implication for Sovereign:** Tension comes from *threatening what the player already has*, not from a separate doom counter. The lever: make Credit/Influence loss **visibly debit the player's own score each turn** when the Republic is sliding. A −2 Influence "interest payment" stings ~2x more than a +2 reward delights — and it's felt *every turn*, not at a far-off threshold.

## 2. Endowment effect: owned assets are overvalued (Kahneman, Knetsch & Thaler; Eichberger et al. 2018)
*"The Endowment Effect in Games," Games & Economic Behavior. https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0014292117300521* — People demand far more to give up what they own than to acquire the same thing.
**Implication:** The failure gauge must *take from the player's pile*, not from an abstract national stat. A shared "Public Credit" track nobody owns generates no dread. Lever: have Public Doubt **siphon the player's accumulated Influence/credit directly** (e.g., each stage skims a % of your stockpile). Now the gauge is eating *your* things.

## 3. Near-miss effect: "almost" recruits win-circuitry and drives engagement (Clark et al., 2009)
*"Gambling Near-Misses Enhance Motivation to Gamble and Recruit Win-Related Brain Circuitry," Neuron 61(3). https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19217383/* — Near-misses are subjectively unpleasant yet light up the ventral striatum like a win and *increase* motivation.
**Implication:** Tension lives in the *almost-crossed* threshold, not the crossed one. Lever: a **telegraphed meter that visibly fills toward the edge and recedes** — so the player repeatedly experiences "I was one tick from Credit Crisis and pulled back." The near-miss, not the catastrophe, is the dopamine. This argues for a fine-grained, always-visible "Republic sliding" bar over discrete stage labels.

## 4. Tension = vulnerability + uncertainty + meaningful failure cost (Chris Pruett, 2011)
*"The Mechanics of Tension," Game Developer. https://www.gamedeveloper.com/design/the-mechanics-of-tension* — Tension requires the player to feel *not in control* and that "the rules may change at any time"; failure must cost real progress.
**Implication:** A deterministic game can't use RNG dread, so it must manufacture *vulnerability*: the player should feel the system can hurt them on the next turn. Lever: a **compounding interest mechanic** — each turn in the danger zone increases next turn's penalty. Determinism becomes the threat ("if I don't act, the math will grind me down") rather than safety.

## 5. The decorative-gauge mechanism — engine compounds, brake doesn't (Pandemic / Eldritch Horror analyses, Meeples Together / Shut Up & Sit Down design notes)
*Pandemic design notes (Medium, Rayen Magpantay) https://medium.com/quick-game-design-notes/pandemic-quick-design-notes-843198535d96 ; Eldritch Horror case study, Meeples Together https://www.meeplestogether.com/2021/03/16/case-study-eldritch-horror/* — Pandemic's diseases spread **exponentially** while player remedies stay **linear**; Eldritch Horror uses a **stratified, pre-stacked Mythos deck** so the game provably gets worse over time. The threat *outpaces* the player on its own.
**Implication — THIS is why Sovereign's gauge is decorative:** Sovereign almost certainly has a *linear, resettable* resistance stat with no self-reinforcing growth, so player actions easily neutralize it and it never reaches the top tiers (exactly the README symptom). Lever: give the failure track a **positive feedback loop** — higher Resistance/Doubt raises the *rate* at which it rises (or raises every action's cost), so neglect compounds. Without compounding, no threshold tuning will ever bite.

## 6. Persistent low-level risk beats rare punishment (risk-reward feedback-loop design; Tarkov/Hades pattern)
*"The Feedback Loop Formula," Playercounter. https://playercounter.com/blog/why-risk-reward-cycles-dominate-modern-game-design/* — Continuous, ever-present low-level tension ("skin in the game" every encounter, as in Escape from Tarkov) sustains engagement far better than occasional fear events.
**Implication:** Don't model failure as a rare boss event; model it as **standing weather**. Lever: a small **per-turn upkeep/debt service** the player must continually outrun. The Republic should cost something to run *every* turn, so the player always has skin in the gauge — never a stretch where failure is irrelevant.

## 7. Cumulative, varied escalation creates climbing pressure (Slay the Spire Ascension)
*Slay the Spire Ascension, Mega Crit. https://slay-the-spire.fandom.com/wiki/Ascension* — Each level stacks *cumulative* modifiers that tweak *different* systems (not just bigger numbers), pushing the player to make near-zero mistakes.
**Implication:** Stages should change *how the game plays*, not just display a worse word. Lever: each failure stage imposes a **distinct, stacking penalty** (Public Doubt: −actions; Credit Crisis: borrowing costs Influence; Panic: opponents gain tempo; Default: hard cap on credit). Stacking + variety = mounting dread that a relabeled single-axis meter can't produce.

## 8. Players accept losing only when defeat is *telegraphed and causal* — else they feel robbed (fairness-perception design)
*"What Makes a Game Feel 'Fair'," GameLuster https://gameluster.com/what-makes-a-game-feel-fair-lessons-from-pvp-rng-and-real-money-play/ ; "The Perception of Fairness," Game Developer https://www.gamedeveloper.com/design/the-perception-of-fairness* — Players "want to trust a defeat was a misplay, not that they were set up to fail"; fairness collapses when outcomes seem disconnected from agency.
**Implication — guards against the death-spiral failure mode:** A compounding drain is only acceptable if the player **saw it building and had levers to stop it**. Lever: surface a **"Republic is sliding" forecast** ("at this rate: Credit Crisis in 2 turns") + a clearly affordable corrective action each turn. This is the *point-of-no-return done right* — the player crosses it by choice/neglect, not by ambush, so loss feels earned, not punitive.

## 9. Power Grid: scarcity makes *every* decision tense via a visible escalating cost track
*Power Grid (Friese, 2004), BGG + Board&Game review. https://boardgamegeek.com/boardgame/2651/power-grid ; https://boardandgame.com/2015/10/05/review-power-grid-...* — Resources sit on a linear track of *escalating* prices that rise as supply is consumed; "almost every decision is tense" because the cost of inaction is always visible and climbing.
**Implication:** Make the *cost of ignoring the Republic* visibly escalate on a track the player watches tick. Lever: a Credit/upkeep cost that **ratchets up the longer Doubt sits high**, displayed as a rising number. The dread is in *watching the price of neglect climb*, deterministically, in plain sight.

---

## Synthesis — which levers, ranked

1. **Add a compounding feedback loop to the failure track (highest leverage).** The decorative problem is structural: a linear/resettable gauge. Make Doubt/Resistance raise its own growth rate or raise every action's cost. (Findings 5, 4, 9.)
2. **Tie failure to the player's OWN score, debited visibly each turn.** Loss aversion + endowment: the gauge must eat *your* Influence/credit, not an abstract national stat, and you must *see the debit*. (Findings 1, 2, 6.)
3. **Replace discrete rarely-crossed stages with an always-visible "Republic sliding" meter + forecast.** Engineer near-misses; let the player repeatedly almost-fail and recover. (Findings 3, 8.)
4. **Make each stage a distinct stacking gameplay penalty, with a telegraphed point-of-no-return.** Cumulative variety = dread; forecast + affordable counter = "earned, not robbed." (Findings 7, 8.)

**What NOT to do (confirmed by research):** Do not just lower thresholds or add more stages on a linear gauge — a threat that "rarely fires" is perceptually nonexistent (Finding 5), and an un-telegraphed compounding drain reads as punitive/unfair (Finding 8).
