# Game Feel / "Juice" / Moment-to-Moment Feedback — for Sovereign (turn-based, deterministic)

Study-swarm finding. Question: what makes a mechanically-correct game *viscerally satisfying* rather than flat, and how does it translate to a TURN-BASED, deterministic, board-game-grammar digital game (not an action game)?

**The core translation insight:** Most "juice" canon was written for real-time action games, but the load-bearing layer — *anticipation → action → exaggerated feedback → consequence/permanence* — is **engine-agnostic**. Sovereign doesn't get screenshake-on-aim; it gets the *turn-based equivalent*: ceremony around state changes. The enemy of Sovereign is not "lack of physics," it is **results that appear without ceremony** and **opponent turns as dead air**.

---

### 1. Swink: game feel = real-time control + simulated space + **polish**. Only "polish" survives the action→turn-based jump.
Steve Swink defines game feel as "real-time control of virtual objects in a simulated space, with interactions emphasized by polish," with a sub-100ms correction cycle (Swink 2008). The first two pillars (real-time control, simulated space/physics) **do not apply** to a deterministic turn-based board game — chasing them is a category error. The third, **polish** ("interactions emphasized by polish without changing the simulation"), is exactly what Sovereign lacks and can fully adopt.
→ **Sovereign:** Stop trying to make the dice/board "feel physical." Pour the budget into polish layered *on top of* the existing deterministic resolution: every transaction, vote, and roll resolves with a brief animated flourish that changes nothing mechanically but confirms the state change viscerally.

### 2. Nijman "Art of Screenshake": the transferable tricks are **hit-pause, anticipation, knockback, permanence, and sound-with-bass** — NOT camera shake.
Nijman's 30 tricks turn a flat platformer juicy (Nijman 2013). Triaged for turn-based: **sleep/pause frames** (freeze a beat on impact), **anticipation** (telegraph before the event), **knockback** (the struck thing visibly reacts), **permanence** (evidence of action persists — shell casings, corpses), and **bass-heavy impact sound** all transfer directly. Screen shake, muzzle flash, strafing, rate-of-fire do not.
→ **Sovereign:** (a) On a *contested auction win* or *failed vote*, insert a 200–400ms hit-pause before the result resolves — the beat of dread. (b) **Knockback:** when you lose an asset or take an economic hit, the affected card/token visibly recoils/flashes red and shrinks. (c) **Permanence:** keep a visible, growing ledger of what each player has done this game (assets seized, acts passed) — the board should *accumulate evidence of history*, not reset to a clean state each turn.

### 3. "Juice it or lose it": juice = surprise & delight via **easing, squash/stretch, scaling, particle bursts, layered sound** on state changes.
Jonasson & Purho live-juiced Breakout from flat to alive using tweening/easing, squash-and-stretch, scaling pops, particles, and stacked sound — all applied to *discrete state changes* (brick destroyed, ball bounced), which is structurally identical to a board-game event (Jonasson & Purho 2012). This is the single most directly applicable talk: **none of their techniques require real-time control.**
→ **Sovereign:** Numbers must never *snap* to a new value. When Influence Points or cash change, **tween the counter** (count up/down over ~500ms) with a scale-pop on the final digit and a coin/gavel sound. Asset tokens scale-pop when bought. The d6 result lands with a small bounce/settle (squash-stretch), not an instant integer.

### 4. The "one-more-turn" engine is **per-turn delta visibility + delayed-reward promises**, not action.
Civ's addictive loop works because "each turn, a few numbers go up or something moves/changes," combined with the game selling "promises of something fancy that will happen if you keep playing" (multiple analyses, 2017–2023). This is purely turn-based and purely about *visualizing change* + *dangling a near-future payoff*. Crucially, **the player must SEE the delta every single turn**, even small ones.
→ **Sovereign:** End every turn with a one-line "this turn" delta card ("+3 IP, +$200, Bank chartered → matures in 2 turns"). Surface **maturing/pending payoffs** explicitly ("National Bank pays out at next Accounting") so the player always has a reason to want the next turn. The Final Accounting should be *visible as a countdown gauge* the whole game.

### 5. Variable vs fixed feedback: **dopamine fires on uncertain anticipation**, not on the reward itself.
Variable-ratio reinforcement produces stronger, more persistent engagement than fixed because dopamine is released during *uncertain anticipation* of reward, not on receipt (Neurolaunch; PMC4318496). The visceral moment is the *reveal*, not the outcome. A deterministic game can still engineer this: event cards, vote outcomes, and auction results are *uncertain to the player even if computed deterministically*.
→ **Sovereign:** Maximize the **reveal beat** on the two genuinely uncertain surfaces — the **2d6 roll** (slow-roll the dice, brief suspense before they settle) and the **event card flip** (card slides in face-down, pauses, then flips with a sound sting). Never resolve these instantly/silently — the anticipation gap *is* the fun, and it costs nothing mechanically.

### 6. Loss aversion: losing feels ~2× as strong as gaining — so **feedback on losses should be the loudest surface in the game.**
Losses are felt roughly twice as intensely as equivalent gains (Kahneman/Tversky, applied to games — Achievement Relocked, MIT Press; GDC Board Game Design Day 1024238). Designers exploit this: framing rewards as *protecting what you have* and making loss tension front-and-center skyrockets engagement.
→ **Sovereign:** When you *lose* an asset, get out-voted, or pay a penalty, give it MORE ceremony than a gain — a red flash, a downward number-tween with a heavier sound, a brief "X seized by [Rival]" banner. Reframe your asset panel as **"holdings to defend"** (what's at risk in the next Accounting), not just a static inventory. This converts a deterministic economic simulation into felt stakes.

### 7. Twilight Struggle digital: **period audio + voice + sound stings = atmosphere**, and it carries an otherwise austere turn-based system.
The TS app is repeatedly praised not for animation but for its **soundtrack with Cold War quotes, leaders speaking, "spy" sounds** that "give it an atmospheric edge" and make "the tension and feel come to life" (Playdek; Digitally Downloaded 2016; 8bit Meeple). The mechanics are dry card-play; the *sound design* is what makes it feel alive. This is the cheapest high-leverage juice for a board-game adaptation.
→ **Sovereign:** Commission/assemble a small library of **period-appropriate audio**: a gavel for votes, a coin-clink for transactions, a quill-scratch for signing Acts, ambient 1790s tavern/chamber murmur under the board, and short orchestral stings on big events. Pair this with the already-researched Chronicler voice. Sound is *the* multiplier for an austere economic table.

### 8. Slay the Spire: **non-blocking animation + intent telegraphing + thresholded visual states** make a turn-based card game feel fast and weighty at once.
StS lets you "cast cards as quickly as you'd like — animations do their own pace, you don't wait" (non-blocking), uses **massive hitboxes** so you never mis-target, shows damage via **changing weapon icons at thresholds** rather than raw numbers ("more visceral than just numbers"), and gives cards "exaggerated effects and sounds" so they "feel like they have weight" (Cloudfall Studios 2018; Gunslingers Revenge). Critically — **enemy intent is telegraphed before it happens**, so opponent turns are readable, not dead air.
→ **Sovereign:** (a) **Never block input on animation** — if the player clicks ahead, fast-forward/queue the flourish; juice must never become a tax on tempo. (b) **Telegraph opponent intent**: before an AI opponent acts, show a one-beat thought-bubble/intent ("Hamilton eyes the Bank auction…") so their turn is a readable mini-drama, not a log dump. (c) Represent asset health/value with **thresholded visual states** (a thriving vs failing business *looks* different) rather than a bare number.

### 9. Anti-pattern: the "spreadsheet that plays itself." Flatness comes from **instant silent resolution + opponent dead air + no per-turn delta.**
Synthesizing the above: a deterministic economic game dies when (a) results appear instantly with no anticipation gap (kills the dopamine-on-anticipation effect, #5), (b) opponent turns are a silent log or a progress bar (dead air — the opposite of StS intent telegraphing, #8), and (c) nothing visibly *changes or accumulates* turn to turn (kills one-more-turn, #4). The incremental-game literature confirms the inverse: "each incrementing number creates a psychological effect reinforcing engagement… rhythmic visual feedback" (techguide; Eric Guan). The fix is not more rules — it is **ceremony, delta-visibility, and animated opponent turns.**
→ **Sovereign:** Treat opponent turns as **the game's best storytelling slot, not dead air** — animate each AI's roll/move/transaction with the same flourishes and a one-line Chronicler narration, so watching rivals scheme is a spectator pleasure. Audit the current build for any state change that happens *silently and instantly* — each one is a flatness bug.
