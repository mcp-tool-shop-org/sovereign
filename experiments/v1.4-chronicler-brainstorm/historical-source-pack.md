## Purpose

This file is the **only authoritative source** for the Chronicler's historical content in v1.4. Quotes, dates, names, vote tallies, and historical anchors not present here must not be attributed to historical figures or framed as documented fact. Invented period-flavored prose is allowed *only* as unattributed Chronicler narration.

## Verification Standard

Every retained item in this file has either:
- A `founders.archives.gov` URL (canonical Federal Era archive),
- A Wikisource URL (Federalist Papers and other primary sources),
- A Wikipedia URL plus at least one academic source citation, or
- A Library of Congress / National Archives reference.

Items the research compilation excluded because verification failed (popular misattributions, Hamilton musical lyrics presented as Hamilton, contested phrasing without source URL) are not in this file. **If Claude Design "remembers" a quote that's not in this file, it MUST NOT be attributed to a historical figure.**

## How to Use This File

1. **Section 1 (Quote Bank)** — every direct quote the Chronicler attributes to a historical figure must come from this section. Select by topic tag and suggested trigger. Quote text is canonical; do not modify wording.
2. **Section 2 (Federal Era Event Captions)** — the always-visible 1-line italic caption on each Federal Era Event banner. Use the suggested caption verbatim or the alt phrasing. Do not invent new ones.
3. **Section 3 (Act Historical Contexts)** — when an Act passes (or fails), the Chronicler banner uses the suggested banner in this section, or derives from it using the documented historical facts.
4. **Section 4 (Credit Tier Analogs)** — when a Credit tier escalates (Public Doubt / Crisis / Panic / Default), use the corresponding tier's suggested banner.
5. **Section 5 (Vision Historical Framing)** — closing-entry framing when a player achieves their profile Vision in the endgame.
6. **Section 6 (Federal Era Opening)** — round-8 transition banner.
7. **Section 7 (Style Rules)** — voice prescriptions to apply to every line of new Chronicler prose written for v1.4.

## Section 2 — Federal Era Event Captions (8 events)

Each Federal Era Event in v1.3 must show one of these captions as an always-visible 1-line italic line on the event banner.

### Event 1: Creditors Demand Payment

- **Caption:** *"In July 1791 the Bank of the United States opened subscriptions in Philadelphia; demand exceeded supply within an hour, and small bondholders found themselves shut out at the door."*
- **Historical anchor:** First Bank of the United States initial public subscription, July 4, 1791. Hamilton's Bank Bill passed February 25, 1791; the initial scrip offering oversubscribed in roughly one hour, igniting "scrippomania" that fed into the Panic of 1792.
- **Sources:** Cowen, David J., "The First Bank of the United States and the Securities Market Crash of 1792," *Journal of Economic History* 60:4 (Dec 2000), pp. 1041-1060; Sylla / Wright / Cowen, "Alexander Hamilton, Central Banker: Crisis Management during the U.S. Financial Panic of 1792," *Business History Review* 83:1 (Spring 2009), pp. 61-86.
- **URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Bank_of_the_United_States
- **Alt phrasing:** *"The subscription books closed at noon on the Fourth of July 1791; the gallery emptied of shouting men, and the small holders walked home with nothing in hand."*

### Event 2: Public Works Petition

- **Caption:** *"In 1796 Congress debated Albert Gallatin's call for federal roads and canals; the petitions piled at the Speaker's table while the appropriation languished in committee."*
- **Historical anchor:** Internal improvements debates of the Federal Era; post road expansions under the Postal Service Act of 1792; Gallatin's emergent advocacy culminating in his 1808 Report.
- **Sources:** Larson, John Lauritz, *Internal Improvement: National Public Works and the Promise of Popular Government in the Early United States* (UNC Press, 2001); John, Richard R., *Spreading the News: The American Postal System from Franklin to Morse* (Harvard, 1995).
- **URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postal_Service_Act
- **Alt phrasing:** *"Memorials from the back-country reached Philadelphia each session — for a road, a bridge, a post — and each session the appropriation was deferred to the next."*

### Event 3: Route Monopoly Inquiry

- **Caption:** *"The Postal Service Act of 1792 placed every post road under congressional charter; private carriers who had run the mail by handshake found their routes opened to inquiry."*
- **Historical anchor:** Postal Service Act, signed February 20, 1792. Established Post Office Department on permanent footing; vested route designation in Congress rather than Postmaster General; ended several private carrier monopolies along Boston-NY-Philadelphia corridor.
- **Sources:** John, Richard R., *Spreading the News* (Harvard, 1995), ch. 1-2; Kielbowicz, Richard B., *News in the Mail* (Greenwood, 1989).
- **URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postal_Service_Act
- **Alt phrasing:** *"After February 1792 no carrier rode the post road by private right; every route ran by act of Congress, and the old contractors petitioned for recourse."*

### Event 4: Workshop Shortage

- **Caption:** *"Hamilton's Report on Manufactures of December 1791 catalogued the want of skilled hands; the mills at Paterson stood half-finished while workmen were sought from Britain in violation of her emigration laws."*
- **Historical anchor:** Hamilton's *Report on the Subject of Manufactures*, December 5, 1791; founding of the Society for Establishing Useful Manufactures (SUM) at Paterson, NJ, 1791. British Acts of 1719 and 1750 forbade artisan emigration; SUM recruited workmen covertly.
- **Sources:** Nelson, John R., *Liberty and Property: Political Economy and Policymaking in the New Nation, 1789-1812* (Johns Hopkins, 1987); Davis, Joseph Stancliffe, *Essays in the Earlier History of American Corporations*, Vol. I (Harvard, 1917).
- **URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Report_on_Manufactures
- **Alt phrasing:** *"In December 1791 the Secretary laid his Report before Congress; the looms at Paterson waited, and the agents in Manchester sent word that the passage would cost more than the men."*

### Event 5: Speculation Inquiry

- **Caption:** *"In March 1792 William Duer's paper failed in New York; the panic ran from Wall Street to State Street within the fortnight, and Hamilton bought into the market with Treasury funds to arrest the fall."*
- **Historical anchor:** Panic of 1792, March-April 1792. William Duer (former Assistant Secretary of the Treasury) cornered BUS scrip on borrowed money; default on March 9, 1792 collapsed prices. Hamilton authorized open-market purchases by the Sinking Fund commissioners and lent specie to the Bank of New York — first US central-bank-style crisis intervention.
- **Sources:** Sylla / Wright / Cowen, "Alexander Hamilton, Central Banker," *Business History Review* 83:1 (Spring 2009), pp. 61-86; Chernow, Ron, *Alexander Hamilton* (Penguin, 2004), ch. 19.
- **URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panic_of_1792
- **Alt phrasing:** *"Duer was carried to the New Gaol on Friday and the market broke on Monday; by week's end the Secretary had ordered the Sinking Fund into the breach."*

### Event 6: Bondholder Demand

- **Caption:** *"Under the Funding Act of 1790 the coupons fell due each quarter; the Treasury counted its specie against the warrants and the bondholders counted theirs against the calendar."*
- **Historical anchor:** Funding Act of August 4, 1790. Quarterly interest payments in specie created recurrent Treasury cash strain through the 1790s. The "deferred sixes" and 3-percent stock established the schedule governing Federal Era public finance.
- **Sources:** Edling, Max M., *A Hercules in the Cradle: War, Money, and the American State, 1783-1867* (Chicago, 2014), ch. 3; Perkins, Edwin J., *American Public Finance and Financial Services, 1700-1815* (Ohio State, 1994).
- **URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Funding_Act_of_1790
- **Alt phrasing:** *"The coupons came due in the spring and in the autumn; the Treasury paid in specie, and the merchants in Philadelphia and Boston marked the day in their ledgers."*

### Event 7: Local Resistance Organizes

- **Caption:** *"Through the summer of 1794 the Mingo Creek committees met in the western counties; the excise on whiskey had passed in 1791, and the back-country signed its resolutions before the militia marched."*
- **Historical anchor:** Whiskey Rebellion, 1791-1794. Mingo Creek Association resolutions of 1794 in Washington County, PA. Hamilton's excise of March 3, 1791 fell hardest on small western distillers; resistance organized through county committees; Bower Hill confrontation July 1794; Washington's Watermelon Army marched autumn 1794.
- **Sources:** Slaughter, Thomas P., *The Whiskey Rebellion: Frontier Epilogue to the American Revolution* (Oxford, 1986); Hogeland, William, *The Whiskey Rebellion* (Scribner, 2006).
- **URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whiskey_Rebellion
- **Alt phrasing:** *"The committees met at Mingo Creek through the summer of 1794; the resolutions were read aloud, signed, and sent east before the President called out the militia."*

### Event 8: Final Republic Reckoning

- **Caption:** *"By February 1798 Robert Morris sat in the Prune Street debtors' apartment in Philadelphia; the financier of the Revolution had outrun his last note, and the Federal Era closed its accounts upon him."*
- **Historical anchor:** Robert Morris's bankruptcy and imprisonment, February 1798 - August 1801. Principal financier of the Revolution and former Superintendent of Finance committed to Prune Street debtors' prison February 16, 1798, owing ~$3 million in land-speculation debts. Released 1801 following Bankruptcy Act of 1800 (first federal bankruptcy statute, repealed 1803).
- **Sources:** Rappleye, Charles, *Robert Morris: Financier of the American Revolution* (Simon & Schuster, 2010); Mann, Bruce H., *Republic of Debtors: Bankruptcy in the Age of American Independence* (Harvard, 2002).
- **URL:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Morris_(financier)
- **Alt phrasing:** *"The Prune Street prison held the man who had paid the Revolution; by 1798 the notes had come round, and the new century's bankruptcy law would not arrive in time to spare him the cell."*

## Section 3 — Act Historical Contexts (7 Acts)

For each Act in Sovereign, the verified real-history backing + a suggested Chronicler banner. When an Act passes (or fails) in game, the banner uses the suggested text below or derives directly from the historical facts cited.

### Act 1 — Funding Act

- **Real name:** Funding Act of 1790 (1 Stat. 138)
- **Date signed:** August 4, 1790 (Washington)
- **What it did:** Refinanced ~$54M federal Revolutionary debt at par via new 6% / deferred 6% / 3% stocks. Established a Sinking Fund. Consolidated credit under Treasury authority.
- **Proponent:** Hamilton (Report on Public Credit, Jan 9, 1790).
- **Opposition:** Madison (urged discrimination between original holders and current speculators).
- **Vote:** House 32-29 on July 26, 1790. Madison's discrimination motion defeated 36-13 on February 22, 1790.
- **Outcome:** Federal credit established; Treasury 6s traded above par in Amsterdam by 1791.
- **Sources:** Elkins & McKitrick, *The Age of Federalism* (1993), Ch. 3-4; Chernow, *Alexander Hamilton* (2004), Ch. 17; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Funding_Act_of_1790
- **Suggested Chronicler banner:**
  *"The Funding Act carries the floor on August 4, 1790. Madison's motion of discrimination — that the soldier and the speculator be paid on different scales — falls 36 to 13 in February; the bonds will be redeemed at par to whomever holds them. The Republic's debt is now its own; the credit of the Treasury begins to find a price in Amsterdam and London."*

### Act 2 — Assumption Act

- **Real name:** Funding Act of 1790, assumption provisions (1 Stat. 138, §§13-21) — same statutory vehicle as Funding
- **Date signed:** August 4, 1790
- **What it did:** Federalized ~$21.5M of state Revolutionary debts. Converted state creditors into federal creditors; removed states as competing fiscal sovereigns.
- **Proponent:** Hamilton.
- **Opposition:** Madison (House floor leader); Patrick Henry of Virginia.
- **Compromise:** Compromise of 1790 — Jefferson's "dinner table bargain" at Maiden Lane, c. June 20, 1790. Madison stood aside; Hamilton delivered Northern votes for the Potomac capital.
- **Vote:** House 34-28 on July 26, 1790 (twice defeated earlier in session).
- **Outcome:** State debts absorbed; Virginia/Maryland ceded the federal district; capital moved to Philadelphia (then Washington in 1800). The Compromise marks the inflection point of the First Party System.
- **Sources:** Elkins & McKitrick (1993), Ch. 4; Chernow (2004), Ch. 18; Cooke, Jacob, "The Compromise of 1790," *William and Mary Quarterly* 27 (1970); https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compromise_of_1790
- **Suggested Chronicler banner:**
  *"Assumption carries 34 to 28 on July 26, 1790 — twice defeated in the spring, now redeemed by the Maiden Lane dinner. Madison stands aside; Jefferson holds the chair; Hamilton secures the state debts. The Potomac receives the capital; the Treasury receives the states' Revolutionary obligations. Jefferson will later call his concurrence the greatest political error of his life."*

### Act 3 — Bank Charter

- **Real name:** Act to Incorporate the Subscribers to the Bank of the United States (1 Stat. 191)
- **Date signed:** February 25, 1791 (Washington, after written cabinet debate)
- **What it did:** Chartered the First Bank of the United States for 20 years, $10M capitalization (one-fifth federal subscription). Authority to issue banknotes, hold federal deposits, act as Treasury fiscal agent. Branches in Boston, NY, Baltimore, Charleston.
- **Proponent:** Hamilton (Report on a National Bank, Dec 13, 1790; "Opinion on the Constitutionality of the Bank," Feb 23, 1791 — foundational doctrine of implied powers).
- **Opposition:** Madison (House floor, Feb 2, 1791, Tenth Amendment grounds); Jefferson; AG Edmund Randolph (cabinet opinions urging veto).
- **Compromise:** No legislative compromise. Constitutional fight resolved by Hamilton's written opinion persuading Washington.
- **Vote:** House 39-20 on February 8, 1791. North for; South against — division now plain.
- **Outcome:** Bank opened Philadelphia Dec 12, 1791; stock oversold within an hour. Doctrine of implied powers later constitutionalized in *McCulloch v. Maryland* (1819). Charter expired 1811.
- **Sources:** Elkins & McKitrick (1993), Ch. 6; McDonald, *Alexander Hamilton* (1979), Ch. 9; Chernow (2004), Ch. 19; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Bank_of_the_United_States
- **Suggested Chronicler banner:**
  *"The Bank Bill clears the House 39 to 20 on February 8, 1791 — North for, South against, the division now plain. Madison invokes the Tenth Amendment; Jefferson and Randolph counsel veto; Hamilton answers in seventeen thousand words on implied powers. Washington signs on February 25. The doctrine that the means may be inferred from the ends has entered the constitutional record."*

### Act 4 — Tariff Schedule

- **Real name:** Tariff Act of 1789 (1 Stat. 24); supplemented by Tariff of 1792
- **Date signed:** July 4, 1789 (second statute the new Congress enacted)
- **What it did:** 5% ad valorem on most imports + enumerated rates to 50% on selected goods. Tonnage duties favored American-built/-owned shipping (6¢/ton domestic vs 50¢/ton foreign). 2.5¢/gallon molasses duty. 1792 supplement raised rates to fund debt service.
- **Proponent:** Madison (House floor sponsor) — revenue-raising posture before the Hamiltonian breach.
- **Opposition:** Northern merchants resisted anti-British tonnage discrimination; Southern planters objected to protective rates.
- **Compromise:** Senate removed Madison's anti-British discrimination; House concurred to secure passage by Independence Day.
- **Vote:** House 31-19 on July 4, 1789.
- **Outcome:** Generated ~90% of federal revenue through 1790s. Established protectionist-revenue duality structuring tariff politics through Civil War.
- **Sources:** Irwin, Douglas, *Clashing over Commerce: A History of US Trade Policy* (2017), Ch. 3; Elkins & McKitrick (1993), Ch. 2; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tariff_of_1789
- **Suggested Chronicler banner:**
  *"The Tariff carries 31 to 19 on July 4, 1789 — Independence Day made fiscal. Madison sponsors the schedule; the Senate strikes his discrimination against British bottoms; the House concurs to clear the bill by sundown. Five per centum ad valorem on most goods, six cents the ton on American keels — the customhouse is the Republic's first reliable purse."*

### Act 5 — Coinage Act

- **Real name:** Coinage Act of 1792 / Mint Act (1 Stat. 246)
- **Date signed:** April 2, 1792
- **What it did:** Established the US Mint at Philadelphia (first federal building under the Constitution). Dollar defined as 371.25 grains pure silver, pegged to Spanish milled dollar. Gold eagles ($10), half/quarter-eagles; silver dollars, halves, quarters, dismes, half-dismes; copper cents/half-cents. Decimal subdivision. Death penalty for Mint debasement.
- **Proponent:** Hamilton (Report on the Establishment of a Mint, Jan 28, 1791); Jefferson (decimal-system advocacy from 1784); David Rittenhouse (first Director).
- **Opposition:** No significant party opposition. Technical disagreement on bimetallic ratio (15:1) and Washington portrait question.
- **Compromise:** House insisted on Washington portrait; Senate substituted emblematic Liberty; House accepted to secure passage. Bimetallic ratio 15:1 silver:gold per Hamilton.
- **Vote:** Specific roll-call not preserved in standard sources; passage recorded without recorded division on key questions.
- **Outcome:** First federal half-dismes struck July 1792 (reportedly from Martha Washington's silver service per Mint tradition). First cents 1793. 15:1 ratio undervalued silver; silver dollars left circulation under Gresham's Law; dollar coinage suspended 1806.
- **Sources:** Taxay, Don, *The U.S. Mint and Coinage* (1966), Ch. 1-3; McDonald (1979), Ch. 9; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coinage_Act_of_1792
- **Suggested Chronicler banner:**
  *"The Mint Act receives the President's hand on April 2, 1792. Hamilton fixes the dollar at 371 and one quarter grains of pure silver — the Spanish milled dollar made native; the bimetallic ratio set at fifteen to one. Liberty, not Washington, shall face the obverse: the Senate's amendment, the House concurs. The Republic begins to strike its own coin."*

### Act 6 — Report on Manufactures

- **Real name:** Report on the Subject of Manufactures (transmitted to the House)
- **Date submitted:** December 5, 1791. Never enacted as single comprehensive statute.
- **What it did:** Hamilton's longest theoretical state paper. Against the Smithian / Physiocratic agricultural preference. Recommended moderate protective tariffs + targeted bounties + invention premiums + raw-materials duty exemption + internal improvements + federal patent posture. Tariff portion largely adopted in Tariff of 1792 (May 2, 1792); bounty system rejected as constitutionally suspect.
- **Proponent:** Hamilton (with Tench Coxe, Assistant Secretary of the Treasury).
- **Opposition:** Jefferson and Madison — bounties as unconstitutional General Welfare Clause reach; concentration of corruption around the Treasury. Society for Establishing Useful Manufactures (SUM) at Paterson collapsed in 1792 (post-Duer panic), blunting momentum.
- **Compromise:** Report was advisory. Implementation partial: tariff portion 1792; bounty stillborn.
- **Vote:** Not voted as unified measure. Tariff of 1792 enacted most schedule recommendations.
- **Outcome:** Tariff doctrines into law; shaped American protective policy through Clay's "American System" and beyond. Bounty doctrine became long American dispute over federal industrial policy. Report widely treated as founding text of American developmental economics.
- **Sources:** Elkins & McKitrick (1993), Ch. 7; McDonald (1979), Ch. 10; Chernow (2004), Ch. 20; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Report_on_Manufactures
- **Suggested Chronicler banner:**
  *"On December 5, 1791, Hamilton lays the Report on Manufactures before the House — the longest of his state papers, drafted in part by Tench Coxe. The tariff schedules will carry; the bounties will not. Jefferson reads corruption in every subsidy; Madison reads a General Welfare Clause grown beyond its hinges. The Report becomes doctrine without ever becoming statute."*

### Act 7 — Excise Enforcement

- **Real name:** Excise Act of March 3, 1791 (1 Stat. 199) — the "Whiskey Tax"
- **Date signed:** March 3, 1791; enforcement crisis culminated 1794
- **What it did:** First federal excise on a domestically produced good (distilled spirits). Favored large eastern distillers (flat annual capacity fee) over small frontier producers (per-gallon rate, specie-only). Funded interest on assumed state debts. Bound federal government to extract revenue from trans-Appalachian economy. Amended 1792 and 1794.
- **Proponent:** Hamilton — treated frontier compliance as load-bearing test of new government authority.
- **Opposition:** Western PA farmer-distillers (Mingo Creek Association, David Bradford, James McFarlane); Gallatin (then moderate PA state legislator); Democratic-Republican press characterized as stamp-tax.
- **Compromise:** 1792/1794 statutory amendments reduced small-distiller burden — insufficient. Washington proclaimed August 7, 1794; federalized ~12,950 militia from PA, NJ, VA, MD — largest federal force until War of 1812.
- **Vote:** House 35-21 on January 27, 1791.
- **Outcome:** ~150 rebels arrested autumn 1794; two convicted of treason, pardoned by Washington 1795. Federal authority asserted by force; rebellion suppressed without battle. Politically the excise became Republican recruiting; Jefferson repealed 1801.
- **Sources:** Slaughter (1986); Elkins & McKitrick (1993), Ch. 9; Chernow (2004), Ch. 27; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whiskey_Rebellion
- **Suggested Chronicler banner:**
  *"The Excise carries the House 35 to 21 on January 27, 1791; Washington signs on March 3. The frontier answers in 1794 — Bradford at Mingo Creek, McFarlane fallen at Bower Hill. Hamilton counsels force; Washington proclaims; near thirteen thousand militia march west of the mountains. No battle is given. The federal authority to tax inland production stands proven; Jefferson will repeal the duty in 1802."*

## Section 4 — Credit Tier Historical Analogs (4 tiers)

### Public Doubt (Credit ≤ 6)

- **Real-history analog:** Pre-Funding-Act bond speculation and Continental currency discount, 1789-July 1790.
- **Key documented facts:** $25M in state war debts at deep discount through 1789; original holders sold to speculators for fractions of face value; "not worth a Continental" entered the language. Certificates jumped to $52.50 at Funding Act passage; $66.83 by October 1790 — meaning they had been trading far lower.
- **Sources:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Funding_Act_of_1790; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Report_on_the_Public_Credit
- **Suggested Chronicler banner (50-80 words):**
  *"The market shows its first tremor. Continental scrip changes hands at a fraction of face; the old soldiers' certificates pass to speculators for pennies on the dollar. Hamilton has not yet laid his Report on the Public Credit on Madison's desk, and the city's coffee houses trade rumor as if it were specie. The Republic's word is on the auction block, and the auctioneers know it."*

### Credit Crisis (Credit ≤ 4)

- **Real-history analog:** Panic of 1792 — William Duer's default, March 9, 1792.
- **Key documented facts:** Duer ceases payments March 9, 1792. Securities prices fall >20% in weeks. Hamilton works with William Seton of the Bank of New York; authorizes purchase of $150,000 in public debt at 7% penalty rate; later commits up to $500,000 in Treasury backing. By April 16, 1792 market demand returns to normal.
- **Sources:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panic_of_1792; Sylla / Wright / Cowen, *Business History Review* 83:1 (2009); Federal Reserve Bank of NY, "Crisis Chronicles" (2014).
- **Suggested Chronicler banner (50-80 words):**
  *"March, 1792. William Duer's house of paper collapses overnight; Sixes plunge twenty percent in a fortnight, and every coffee-house clerk on Wall Street is a creditor with nothing to show. Hamilton writes William Seton at the Bank of New York with the first lender-of-last-resort instruction ever drafted in this Republic: buy on government account, at penalty rate, against any good collateral. The credit holds — barely."*

### Panic (Credit ≤ 2)

- **Real-history analog:** Robert Morris's collapse and imprisonment, February 1798, deepening through Panic of 1796-1797.
- **Key documented facts:** Morris — Superintendent of Finance, signer of the Declaration, PA senator, richest man in America in 1775 — committed to Prune Street debtors' apartment February 1798, owing nearly $3M. Held 1798 to August 1801. North American Land Company's collapse pulled credit out of every speculative venture from NY to KY. Bond and loan markets froze.
- **Sources:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Morris_(financier); Rappleye (2010); Mann (2002).
- **Suggested Chronicler banner (50-80 words):**
  *"The financier of the Revolution sits in Prune Street, owing three millions he cannot pay. Robert Morris — who once pledged his own credit for Washington's army — has dragged the land market down behind him, and no broker on Chestnut Street will write a bond against any collateral. The Bank's directors have shut the discount window. There is no loan to be had at any rate. The Republic learns what a frozen credit market costs."*

### Default (Credit = 0)

- **Real-history analog:** NO sovereign default occurred in the Federal Era. The FEAR was real: Shays's Rebellion (August 1786 - February 1787); pre-Constitution debt repudiation pressure; the specter that drove Hamilton's whole assumption-and-funding architecture.
- **Key documented facts:** Daniel Shays led ~1,500 armed men against the federal arsenal at Springfield on January 25, 1787. Four insurgents killed by Massachusetts militia artillery. Federal government under Articles could not finance troops to suppress it. Hamilton and Madison cited the rebellion as evidence the Articles must be replaced.
- **Important note:** Default never realized in 1789-1801. The Chronicler frame is the feared outcome the Hamiltonian system was designed to prevent.
- **Sources:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shays%27s_Rebellion; Mount Vernon Digital Encyclopedia; Bill of Rights Institute.
- **Suggested Chronicler banner (50-80 words):**
  *"What never happened — and what the architects feared above all. Shays's men marched on Springfield in '87 because a state could not pay its veterans, and a Confederation could not raise a regiment to stop them. Hamilton built the funded debt, the Bank, and the excise as a wall against this hour: the day the Republic's word would be worth nothing, and every holder of a federal Six would learn that paper is only as good as the power behind it."*

## Section 5 — Profile Vision Historical Framing (3 visions)

### Federal Credit Architect (Treasury / Finance)

- **In-game conditions:** Credit ≥ 8 + Bank Charter passed or BUS owned + diverse finance holdings (1 Rev-Debt + 1 State-Debt + 1 NF).
- **Real-history analog:** Hamilton's full credit system in operation by 1791-1795. Funding Act (Aug 4, 1790) + BUS chartered (Feb 25, 1791) + Report on Manufactures (Dec 5, 1791) + Excise (Mar 3, 1791). By 1795 federal six-percents traded near par in Amsterdam and London.
- **Sources:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Bank_of_the_United_States; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Funding_Act_of_1790; EH.net; Federal Reserve History.
- **Suggested Chronicler closing line (50-80 words):**
  *"This is the Republic Hamilton drafted on paper in January of 1790 and built in stone by 1795: a funded debt that strangers trust, a Bank with a twenty-year national charter, an excise that fills the Treasury, and a Mint that strikes coin in the Republic's own name. The Sixes trade at par in Amsterdam. The architect of public credit signs their ledger and closes the book on doubt."*

### Commerce Sovereign (Merchant / Infrastructure)

- **In-game conditions:** 2+ routes + 1+ Commercial Infrastructure + 5+ broker/route income.
- **Real-history analog:** Robert Morris's pre-collapse commercial empire and the rise of Philadelphia / NY merchant houses in the 1790s. Willing & Morris (one of the largest trading firms in the colonies) survived into the early Republic as Thomas Willing's house; Willing became first president of BUS in 1791.
- **Key documented facts:** Morris and Willing partnered 1757. Trade ties to Britain, Portugal, Spain, West Indies, Levant, China, Africa. Among first colonial insurers of cargo. Morris richest man in America by 1775. Willing first BUS president 1791.
- **Sources:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Morris_(financier); Mount Vernon Digital Encyclopedia; UPenn Archives.
- **Suggested Chronicler closing line (50-80 words):**
  *"From the Philadelphia wharves where Willing and Morris first opened a Levant route to the New York counting-houses now insuring China cargoes — this is the merchant Republic the 1790s built. Holds, routes, brokers, warehouses; manifest in one hand and bill of exchange in the other. The richest man in America in '75 took the same road, and Thomas Willing now sits as president of the Bank. The commerce sovereign closes their ledger in profit."*

### Industrial Founder (Manufacturer / Industry)

- **In-game conditions:** Capacity ≥ 7 + 3+ Mfg/Strategic + 1+ Mfg/Strategic upgrade.
- **Real-history analog:** Society for Establishing Useful Manufactures (SUM, founded 1791, Paterson NJ — Hamilton-backed industrial society) + Samuel Slater's mill at Pawtucket RI (water-powered spinning began Dec 20, 1790; new mill opened 1793).
- **Key documented facts:** SUM founded 1791, brainchild of Tench Coxe, championed by Hamilton, $500,000+ startup capital, L'Enfant designed the raceways, Great Falls of the Passaic. Slater's Pawtucket mill cited by Hamilton in Report on Manufactures (Dec 5, 1791) as the first American cotton mill. SUM stumbled after Duer embezzled funds in 1792 collapse; pivoted to leasing sites; by 1815, thirteen water-powered cotton mills operated at the Falls.
- **Sources:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Society_for_Establishing_Useful_Manufactures; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slater_Mill; Library of Congress "Today in History" Dec 20; Hamilton Partnership for Paterson.
- **Suggested Chronicler closing line (50-80 words):**
  *"Hamilton laid the Report on Manufactures before Congress in December of '91 and named Slater's mill at Pawtucket as proof the Republic could spin its own thread. The Society at the Great Falls of the Passaic — L'Enfant's raceways, Coxe's charter, half a million in subscribed capital — was the model. This is the founder who built upgraded works in steel and rope and powder, who turned capacity into industry, and who proved Hamilton right where Duer could not."*

## Section 7 — Style Rules for Chronicler Voice

Apply these rules to every line of Chronicler prose written for v1.4 (banners, captions, framings, closing entries).

### USE

- **Latinate abstract nouns** drawn from the Federalist Papers and Hamilton's reports: *establishment, consequence, apprehension, consolidation, credit, calibration, the Republic, the manufactories, the public faith, prudence, posterity, recourse, subscription, demand*.
- **Concrete period referents:** *the bank, the assumption, the tariff, the floor, the cabinet, the customhouse, the militia, the post road, the coffee houses, the back-country*.
- **Semicolon-joined parallel triplets:** "Hamilton secures the bank; Madison loses the floor; the credit of the Republic holds."
- **Periodic sentence structure** (subordinate clause first, main verb late): "Should the assumption fail, the manufactories must wait."
- **Deliberative framing:** "It is the consequence of the season that…"
- **Present tense throughout** mid-game banners: "The Republic answers; the markets calibrate; the season turns."
- **Real dates** when documented: "On August 4, 1790…"

### AVOID

- **Renaissance-era archaisms:** *thee, thou, hath, doth, verily, forsooth, 'tis*. These were already archaic by 1790; they read as Renaissance faire, not Federalist.
- **Modern colloquialisms:** *gonna, sort of, deal with, figure out, kind of, big-time, awesome, super, basically*.
- **Simple SVO sequences:** "Hamilton won. Madison lost. Money came in." Replace with semicolon-joined or periodic structures.
- **Exclamation marks and rhetorical questions** (anachronistically broadcaster-flavored).
- **Mixed registers within the library** — one tonal break is visible to attentive players. Voice consistency across all surfaces.
- **Second-person address to the player** ("you must consider…"). The Chronicler observes the Republic, not the reader.
- **Modern corporate phrasing:** *stakeholders, optimization, telemetry, framework, leverage (as verb), strategic synergy, scalable*.
- **Invented quotes attributed to real figures.** Direct quotation only from Section 1.

### Good voice examples (period-flavored, readable)

- "The Chronicler notes: Madison wrote in *Federalist 10* that 'the regulation of these various and interfering interests forms the principal task of modern legislation.' The Funding Act now puts that claim to its first test."
- "March, 1792. William Duer's house of paper collapses overnight; Sixes plunge twenty percent in a fortnight."
- "Liberty, not Washington, shall face the obverse: the Senate's amendment, the House concurs."

### Bad voice examples (avoid)

- "Ye olde Republic trembles before its sovereign destiny." *(faux-archaic gibberish — wrong era)*
- "The system meets its consequences." *(corporate jargon)*
- "The player's macroeconomic strategy has entered a new phase." *(breaks fourth wall + corporate)*
- "Hamilton was like, totally cooking with this Bank thing." *(modern colloquial)*
